Guided Transmission Media

Twisted-Pair Cable Wired

  • It consists of pairs of insulated copper wires twisted around each other in a spiral layout.
  • The wires are twisted to eliminate outside electrical noise and structural wire-to-wire interference (**crosstalk**).
  • It connects to host network interfaces using a standard 8-pin plastic **RJ-45 connector**.
  • **Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP):** It features a basic outer plastic jacket with no internal metal shields. It is highly affordable and flexible.
  • **Shielded Twisted Pair (STP):** Every wire pair is wrapped inside a protective metal foil layer to block extreme motor noise. It is heavy and stiff.
  • **Categories:** Cat 1 handles basic telephone lines. Cat 5 manages speeds up to 100 Mbps. Cat 6 supports modern speeds up to 1 Gbps.
🏆 Advantages
It is the most economical cable type for local networks, lightweight, and very easy to strip, cut, and deploy.
⚠️ Disadvantages
It suffers from high attenuation values. The signal drops out completely past 100 meters without active repeaters.

Coaxial Cable (COAX) Wired

  • A coaxial cable carries high-frequency electrical signals along four concentric layers sharing one axis.
  • The central copper conductor wire is the component that transmits the actual data currents.
  • The solid white plastic insulator ring keeps the central core perfectly isolated.
  • The braided outer metal mesh shield acts as a grounding loop and blocks radio noise.
  • It secures into target electronics ports using a twist-locking metallic plug called a **BNC connector**.
  • **Thinnet:** It is a thin, flexible cable variant that covers structural distances up to 185 meters.
  • **Thicknet:** It is a thick, rigid trunk line that spans up to 500 meters and acts as a central network backbone.
🏆 Advantages
It provides higher overall bandwidth capacities and covers longer physical distances than twisted-pair cables.
⚠️ Disadvantages
It is heavy and rigid to handle. If the main central backbone trunk breaks, the entire network drops.

Fiber-Optic Cable Wired

  • This is an advanced cable network made of pure glass or plastic strands as thin as a human hair.
  • It transmits network information using high-speed **pulses of light** instead of standard electrical currents.
  • It operates on the physics principle of **Total Internal Reflection (TIR)** where light reflections bounce inside core walls.
  • It features an internal core glass lane, a mirror-finished outer cladding layer, and a protective plastic shock jacket.
  • It uses cheap **LEDs** for short building connections and high-power **Lasers** for transatlantic undersea routes.
  • It interfaces into clean commercial routing nodes via specialized locking hardware plugs named **ST and SC connectors**.
🏆 Advantages
It offers unmatched gigabit bandwidth speeds, long cross-country reaches, and complete immunity to all electrical noise.
⚠️ Disadvantages
It carries a high material purchase cost, is fragile to bend, and requires specialized tools and experts to splice lines.

Unguided Transmission Media

Microwave Transmission Wireless

  • Microwave systems send high-frequency wireless electromagnetic radio signals through the atmosphere.
  • These waves travel strictly along direct, straight lines and cannot wrap around mountains or the Earth’s curve.
  • Because of this straight directional travel, they require a strict **Line-of-Sight (LoS)** alignment between links.
  • Large parabolic dish antennas are mounted on top of tall steel towers, hills, or skyscrapers to maintain sight lines.
  • Due to the curved surface of the Earth, repeating amplification towers must be constructed every 25 to 30 miles.
🏆 Advantages
It avoids the expensive logistics of purchasing land pathways, digging trenches, or laying thousands of miles of wires.
⚠️ Disadvantages
Wireless signals are prone to environmental fading. Heavy downpours, thick winter fog, or blizzards block data streams.

Satellite Communication Wireless

  • A satellite functions as an advanced microwave repeater platform stationed out in deep space.
  • The system is parked inside a geostationary orbit roughly 36,000 kilometers directly above the Earth’s equator line.
  • It matches the exact rotational velocity of the Earth, which makes it look perfectly stationary from any ground base.
  • **The Uplink:** A large ground base station dish on Earth beams a high-frequency radio carrier signal up to the orbit path.
  • **The Transponder:** The satellite system intercepts the weak beam, filters out atmospheric noise, and amplifies its energy.
  • **The Downlink:** The satellite sends the boosted beam back down to Earth over an entire country or continent footprint.
🏆 Advantages
It provides absolute worldwide coverage and connects isolated islands, cruise ships, and desert research outposts easily.
⚠️ Disadvantages
The huge trip distance causes signal delay (**latency**). Launch costs are massive and in-space hardware repairs are impossible.
