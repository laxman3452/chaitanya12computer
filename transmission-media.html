Guided Transmission Media

Guided media channels data signals along a specific physical path using solid wiring. Here is an in-depth breakdown of the three primary types:

1. Twisted-Pair Cable

Consists of pairs of insulated copper wires twisted together. Twisting the wires helps reduce crosstalk and external electromagnetic interference by ensuring that both wires receive the same noise, which can then be canceled out at the receiving end.

Advantages:
  • The most cost-effective cabling option for local networks.
  • Highly flexible, lightweight, and easy to terminate using standard RJ-45 connectors.
Disadvantages:
  • High attenuation limits reliable runs to roughly 100 meters without repeaters.
  • Lower overall bandwidth capacity compared to fiber-optic cables.

2. Coaxial Cable (COAX)

Features a central copper conductor wrapped in a thick insulating layer, surrounded by a braided metal shield to block noise, and finished with a durable outer plastic jacket. Commonly used for cable TV and high-frequency applications using BNC connectors.

Advantages:
  • Higher bandwidth and longer transmission distances than twisted-pair.
  • Excellent noise resistance thanks to its thick concentric outer metal shielding.
Disadvantages:
  • Thick, rigid, heavy, and more difficult to route through tight spaces.
  • A break in a central backbone line can take down the entire connected network segment.

3. Fiber-Optic Cable

An advanced transmission medium made of glass or plastic strands that transmits data as light pulses using Total Internal Reflection (TIR). It offers massive bandwidth and is completely immune to electrical noise.

Advantages:
  • Extremely high speed and massive bandwidth capacity.
  • Total immunity to electromagnetic interference (EMI).
  • Low signal loss allows long distances without repeaters.
Disadvantages:
  • High cost to purchase, install, and configure.
  • Fragile glass cores can snap if bent past their minimum radius.
  • Requires specialized fusion splicing equipment for installation and repairs.

Unguided Transmission Media

Unguided media transmits electromagnetic signals through open space or air without using physical cables. Here is an in-depth breakdown of the primary types:

1. Microwave Systems

Transmits high-frequency radio waves across land. Because these waves travel in straight lines, they require a strict Line-of-Sight (LoS) path between parabolic dish antennas mounted on tall towers or hillsides.

Advantages:
  • Eliminates the need to lay physical cables across challenging terrain.
  • Capable of carrying large amounts of voice and video data over long distances.
Disadvantages:
  • Signal strength can be affected by weather conditions like heavy rain or fog.
  • Constructing and maintaining towers can be expensive.

2. Satellite Communication

Functions as a space-based microwave relay station. Ground antennas beam signals up to a geosynchronous satellite (Uplink), which amplifies the signal and beams it back down to ground stations across a wide area (Downlink).

Advantages:
  • Provides broad coverage across countries or continents.
  • Ideal for remote locations where running cables is impractical.
Disadvantages:
  • The long travel distance to orbit introduces noticeable propagation delay (latency).
  • High upfront costs to build, launch, and manage satellite payloads.
